**Problem:** The paper repeatedly cites "Li und Zhao (2025)" as empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the Chinese ETS. A publication dated 2025 cannot currently serve as published empirical evidence. This is a significant academic integrity issue.

### Issue 2: Overclaim of CBAM Effectiveness
**Location:** Section 1.4, Section 3.1.3, Section 5.4
**Claim:** The text presents the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) as a mechanism that "soll dieses Problem nun auf eine neue Weise adressieren" (will now address this problem in a new way) and aims to "reduzieren" (reduce) carbon leakage.
**Problem:** While CBAM is designed to counter carbon leakage, the text presents it almost as a definitive solution or an established effective mechanism without critically discussing its own potential challenges, complexities, or unproven effectiveness given its very recent implementation (currently in transitional phase with reporting obligations only). There's an implication of proven efficacy that isn't yet warranted.
**Evidence:** CBAM is still a new and evolving policy. Its full economic, administrative, and political impacts (e.g., WTO compatibility, administrative burden for companies, potential for retaliatory measures) are yet to be rigorously studied and confirmed in practice. These potential challenges are not discussed.
**Fix:** Acknowledge CBAM as an *attempt*, a *new policy instrument*, or a *mechanism under development* to mitigate carbon leakage. Crucially, add a critical discussion of its inherent challenges, complexities, and the need for further evaluation of its actual effectiveness and broader impacts.
**Severity:** ðŸ”´ High - Presents a new policy as more established or effective than current evidence allows, leading to an overclaim and an incomplete analysis.

### Issue 3: Premature and Undifferentiated Claims for China ETS Effectiveness
**Location:** Section 3.3.2, Section 5.1
**Claim:** The paper states that "erste Studien positive Auswirkungen auf die Emissionsreduktion und die FÃ¶rderung grÃ¼ner Innovationen" and "bestÃ¤tigen den positiven Einfluss auf die CO2-Emissionen und die technologische Entwicklung" for the national Chinese ETS.
**Problem:** The national Chinese ETS only officially started in July 2021 and initially covered only the power sector. Drawing strong, general conclusions about its "effectiveness" and "positive impacts" based on "erste Studien" (some even future-dated, see Issue 1) is premature. The text doesn't sufficiently differentiate between findings from earlier regional pilot projects (which have a longer history) and the very nascent national system.
**Evidence:** The system's young age and limited initial scope mean that long-term, comprehensive effects on "Emissionsreduktion" and "grÃ¼ne Innovationen" for the entire Chinese economy are not yet discernible or broadly verifiable. Such claims require more mature data.
**Fix:** Qualify these claims explicitly as *preliminary findings* or *based primarily on experiences from pilot projects* where applicable. Clearly state the limitations due to the national system's young age and initial scope. Avoid definitive language regarding broad or long-term effectiveness. If specific quantitative data is available for early results, present it with appropriate context, or explicitly state that detailed quantitative data for the national system's full impact is still emerging.
**Severity:** ðŸ”´ High - Weak arguments due to insufficient evidence and overclaims based on limited or undifferentiated data, potentially misleading the reader.

**Location:** Section 1.1, paragraph 2

**Problem:** While the caveat "sofern das Cap adÃ¤quat festgelegt wird" (provided the cap is adequately set) is present, the challenge of "adequate setting" is a major, complex political, economic, and technical undertaking. The text presents the "guarantee" somewhat strongly without sufficiently discussing the practical difficulties and political sensitivities involved in setting an "adequate" cap, which can lead to over-allocation (as seen in early EU ETS phases) and thus undermine this guarantee in practice.
**Fix:** Expand on the inherent challenges of setting an "adequate" cap, including the political economy of cap-setting (e.g., industry lobbying), the scientific uncertainties in projecting future emissions, and the risk of over-allocation, which can effectively nullify the "guarantee" in practice. This would provide a more balanced theoretical discussion.

### Issue 6: Lack of Quantitative Detail for China ETS
**Location:** Section 3.3.2, Section 5.1

**Fix:** If specific quantitative data from "erste Studien" or pilot projects (clearly distinguished) is available, include it to strengthen the claims. If such detailed data for the national system's early phase is not yet widely available, explicitly state that detailed quantitative data is still emerging, reinforcing the preliminary nature of the findings.

**Logic:** "Chinese national ETS is young (started 2021), initially limited to power sector" + "erste Studien show positive effects (some even future-dated)" â†’ "The system is effective in emissions reduction and green innovation."
**Missing:** A more cautious and nuanced interpretation that explicitly acknowledges the very limited time frame for analysis, the initial narrow scope of the system, and the potential for these "positive effects" to be short-term, specific to early adopters, or influenced by factors other than the ETS itself, rather than being indicative of robust, long-term, systemic change. ## Methodological Concerns (as represented in the paper)

### Concern 1: Attribution of Effects in Young Systems
**Issue:** The paper attributes "positive effects" to the very young national Chinese ETS without fully detailing how these effects are robustly isolated from other concurrent strong policy initiatives (e.g., renewable energy targets, energy efficiency mandates) or general economic trends in a rapidly developing economy.
**Risk:** The reported effects might not be solely attributable to the ETS but could be significantly influenced by other confounding factors that are challenging to control for in "erste Studien" within such a short timeframe. This raises questions about the strength of causal claims.
**Reviewer Question:** "How do the cited 'erste Studien' for the Chinese ETS rigorously control for confounding factors and isolate the specific causal impact of the ETS, given its recent implementation and the dynamic nature of China's economic and environmental policy landscape?"
**Suggestion:** Add a brief discussion of the specific methodological challenges and approaches used by the cited studies (e.g., Shi et al., Zhang et al.) to establish causality for a system in its early stages, or acknowledge this as a current limitation of the evidence base.

## Missing Discussions

1.  **Specific Challenges of CBAM Implementation:** Beyond general statements about countering carbon leakage, a deeper discussion on the practical difficulties, administrative burdens for companies (both EU and non-EU), and potential trade disputes or geopolitical implications arising from CBAM would provide a more complete and critical analysis.
2.  **Trade-offs in Cap-Setting:** While the importance of an "adequate Cap" is mentioned, the inherent trade-offs (e.g., between environmental ambition, economic competitiveness, energy security, and political feasibility) in setting and continuously adjusting the cap are not fully explored. This would enrich the discussion on the practical challenges of ETS design.
3.  **Detailed Arguments on Speculation:** A more comprehensive discussion of the arguments for and against financial speculation in carbon markets would be beneficial. This should include how it might contribute to price discovery and market liquidity, versus its potential for increasing volatility, market manipulation, or diverting capital from real emission reduction investments.
