### Issue 1: Overwhelming Lack of Specific Citations (Academic Integrity)

**Claim:** All claims, empirical findings, theoretical assertions, and historical facts.
**Problem:** The vast majority of statements, including factual assertions, statistics, and theoretical concepts, are currently unsupported by specific academic references. The numerous `{cite_MISSING}` placeholders indicate a critical gap in academic rigor. **Evidence:** The document contains 25 `{cite_MISSING}` placeholders, meaning almost every paragraph relies on unverified information. **Fix:** Systematically research and integrate specific, verifiable academic sources (peer-reviewed journal articles, reputable institutional reports, books) for *every* claim, statistic, and theoretical concept. Ensure all citations include DOIs or arXiv IDs where available. This is the single most important revision.

## Methodological Concerns

### Concern 1: Depth of Discussion on Limitations of Impact Measurement
**Issue:** While the paper correctly acknowledges the complexities and challenges in quantifying ETS impact (1.1.2, 1.5.1), it could delve slightly deeper into the inherent limitations of the econometric methods mentioned (e.g., data availability, potential for omitted variable bias, difficulties in establishing a robust counterfactual, challenges in capturing long-term behavioral changes or systemic shifts).
**Risk:** The discussion, while acknowledging complexity, might still inadvertently convey a higher degree of certainty about measured impacts than is warranted by the methodologies themselves.
**Reviewer Question:** "Beyond naming methods like Difference-in-Differences, what are the specific, acknowledged limitations or ongoing academic debates regarding the robustness and generalizability of these methods in definitively isolating ETS effects, especially when intertwined with numerous other climate policies and economic factors?"
**Suggestion:** Add a brief paragraph or expand existing sentences to critically discuss the inherent uncertainties, methodological debates, or known limitations that persist even with sophisticated econometric techniques used to measure ETS impact.

## Missing Discussions

1.  **Detailed Social Equity and Just Transition Implications:** While "Verteilungswirkungen" are briefly touched upon (1.3.1.2, 1.5.3), a more dedicated and in-depth discussion on how different ETS designs (e.g., free allocation vs. auctioning, specific uses of auction revenues) impact social equity, energy poverty, vulnerable communities, and the broader principles of a just transition would significantly enhance the analysis.
2.  **Political Economy of ETS Design and Reforms:** The paper describes the evolution and reforms of ETS (e.g., EU ETS phases, MSR), but a more explicit discussion of the political economy factors, stakeholder pressures, lobbying efforts, and negotiation processes that shape these designs and reforms would add considerable depth. Why did certain reforms take so long? What were the political trade-offs?
3.  **Future Outlook and Emerging Challenges:** Beyond the current phase of ETS, a brief discussion on future trends, emerging challenges (e.g., integration of new technologies like carbon capture, geopolitical shifts impacting energy markets, the role of negative emissions technologies), and the long-term vision for ETS (e.g., global carbon pricing mechanisms) would be valuable.
4.  **Interactions between ETS and other global carbon pricing initiatives:** While Article 6 is mentioned, the paper could elaborate more on the complexities of different carbon pricing instruments (ETS, taxes, internal carbon prices) interacting across different jurisdictions and the challenges for international harmonization and competitiveness beyond just CBAM.
