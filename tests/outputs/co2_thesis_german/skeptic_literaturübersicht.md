## Logical Gaps

### Gap 1: Causal Link between Kyoto Challenges and EU ETS Success (or lack thereof)
**Location:** Section 2.1 "Die Implementierung dieser Mechanismen war jedoch mit Herausforderungen verbunden." followed by Section 2.2 "Basierend auf den Erfahrungen und der Notwendigkeit, die Kyoto-Ziele zu erreichen, fÃ¼hrte die EuropÃ¤ische Union 2005 den EuropÃ¤ischen Emissionshandel (EU ETS) ein."
**Logic:** The text states the EU ETS was "based on experiences" from Kyoto's challenges. However, it doesn't explicitly link *which* specific Kyoto challenges informed *which* specific design choices in the early EU ETS, or how successful the EU ETS was in overcoming those specific Kyoto-era problems in its early phases.
**Missing:** A more explicit discussion of the lessons learned from Kyoto (e.g., "Kyoto's issues with additionality and project validation highlighted the need for a more centralized, cap-and-trade system like the EU ETS, which directly regulates major emitters rather than relying solely on project-based mechanisms").
**Fix:** Strengthen the causal link between Kyoto's shortcomings and the design evolution of the EU ETS.

### Gap 2: Connection between Coase Theorem and Practical ETS Design
**Location:** Section 3.2 "Coase-Theorem und Eigentumsrechte"
**Logic:** The section states, "Der Emissionshandel kann als eine praktische Anwendung dieses Prinzips verstanden werden." This is a common interpretation, but the text doesn't sufficiently explain *how* the Coase theorem's conditions (clear property rights, zero transaction costs) are approximated or explicitly *violated* in real-world ETS, leading to the necessity of government intervention (like setting the cap, MSR, etc.) rather than purely private bargaining.
**Missing:** A brief acknowledgement that real-world ETS are imperfect Coasian markets due to high transaction costs, information asymmetry, and the need for a central authority to define and enforce property rights (the permits).
**Fix:** Add a sentence or two to clarify the practical limitations of applying the Coase theorem directly to large-scale environmental problems, and how ETS design attempts to overcome some of these, yet still requires significant institutional frameworks.

## Methodological Concerns

### Concern 1: Over-reliance on "cite_XXX" without Contentual Verification
**Issue:** As highlighted in Major Issue 2, the current `cite_XXX` format prevents direct verification of whether the cited source actually supports the claim made in the text, or if it's accurately represented. This is a critical methodological flaw in the review process itself.
**Risk:** Potential for misinterpretation of sources, overstatement of findings, or even unintentional "hallucination" if the underlying sources are not robust.
**Reviewer Question:** "Does `cite_026` truly confirm 'signifikante Emissionsreduktionen' across *all* ETS, or does it present a more nuanced picture with specific conditions and limitations?"
**Suggestion:** Beyond providing DOIs, the author should re-read the cited sections of each paper and ensure their summary accurately reflects the source's findings and caveats.

### Concern 2: Selection Bias in Empirical Studies (Implicit)
**Issue:** While a literature review cannot cover *all* studies, the section on "Empirische Studien" primarily focuses on positive outcomes (emission reduction, innovation) and then addresses negative economic impacts as "potential" or "moderate." There's less emphasis on studies that might find *no* significant effect, or even counterproductive effects, under certain conditions.
**Risk:** The review might inadvertently present an overly optimistic or one-sided view of carbon pricing effectiveness.
**Question:** "Are there studies that have found limited or no effectiveness of carbon pricing in specific contexts, and how are those findings reconciled or addressed?"
**Fix:** Briefly acknowledge that the empirical evidence is not uniformly positive across all contexts and studies, and that some studies highlight conditions where carbon pricing's effectiveness is limited or outweighed by negative impacts. This adds balance.
